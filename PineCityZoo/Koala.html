KOALA

Koalas are tree-dwelling marsupials native to Australia and are easily recognised by their round, fluffy ears, large noses, and thick grey fur. They spend most of their lives in eucalyptus trees, feeding almost exclusively on eucalyptus leaves which are low in nutrients and toxic to many animals. To cope, koalas have a specialised digestive system that breaks down toxins and extracts nutrients slowly. They sleep up to 18 hours a day to conserve energy, as their diet provides limited calories. Koalas were once widespread along Australia’s eastern and southern coasts but have faced habitat loss due to urban expansion and deforestation.

Koalas are solitary and territorial, with each adult occupying a home range that contains several eucalyptus trees. They communicate through vocalisations such as bellows, which can carry over long distances during breeding season. Mothers carry their young, called joeys, in a pouch for about six months before the joey transitions to riding on her back. Koalas have sharp claws and strong limbs, making them excellent climbers. Conservation programs focus on protecting native habitats and managing diseases that threaten wild populations. Koalas are iconic symbols of Australia’s unique wildlife.

Koalas prefer open eucalyptus woodlands with plenty of mature trees for feeding and resting.
