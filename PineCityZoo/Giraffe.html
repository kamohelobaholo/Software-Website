Giraffe

Giraffes are the tallest land animals on Earth, instantly recognisable by their long necks and distinctive spotted coats. Their necks allow them to browse leaves and shoots high up in trees, mostly feeding on acacia species. A giraffe’s tongue can be up to 45 centimetres long and is tough enough to handle thorny branches. Giraffes live in loose herds without strong social bonds, and their keen eyesight helps them keep watch for predators like lions. Their height also makes them an important species for shaping vegetation in their habitat.

Each giraffe’s coat pattern is unique, much like a human fingerprint, and helps with camouflage in dappled light. Despite their size, giraffes move gracefully and can run up to 60 km/h over short distances. Males often compete in “necking” battles, swinging their necks and heads to establish dominance. Female giraffes give birth standing up, and newborn calves can stand and walk within hours. Giraffes are listed as vulnerable due to habitat loss and fragmentation but are protected in many reserves and zoos worldwide.

Giraffes inhabit savannahs, open woodlands, and grasslands with plenty of trees.
