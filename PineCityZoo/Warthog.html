WARTHOG

Warthogs are wild members of the pig family found across the savannahs and grasslands of sub-Saharan Africa. They are easily recognised by their large curved tusks, flat snouts, and facial “warts,” which are actually protective pads. Warthogs spend much of their time grazing on grasses, roots, berries, and bark, often kneeling on their front knees to dig for food with their snouts. They are diurnal and rely on speed and burrows to escape predators like lions and leopards. Warthogs use abandoned aardvark holes for shelter and protection.

Both male and female warthogs have tusks, which they use for digging, fighting, and self-defense. They live in family groups called sounders, usually consisting of females and their young, while males are more solitary. Warthogs are surprisingly fast and can reach speeds of up to 50 km/h when threatened. Despite their tough appearance, they have playful personalities and are known to run with their tails pointed straight up as a signal to other warthogs. They play an important role in the ecosystem by aerating the soil as they dig for food.

Warthogs prefer open grasslands, savannahs, and lightly wooded areas near water.
